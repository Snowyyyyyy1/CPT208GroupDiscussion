01
Research

Understanding the Problem Space

We began by investigating how students and professionals experience group discussions — both in-person and online. Our goal was to uncover real pain points before designing any solution.

Methods used: User interviews (n=8), contextual observation, affinity mapping, and a competitive analysis of existing tools such as Google Meet, Miro, and Discord.

"I always feel like the loudest person dominates — quiet members never get a real turn." — Interview participant, age 21

Key insights discovered:

Unequal speaking time No shared agenda Action items lost post-meeting Difficulty reaching consensus Off-topic drift

Ideation

From Insights to Ideas

Armed with our research findings, we ran two brainstorming sessions using How Might We statements. We generated over 40 concepts across three HMW prompts, then used dot-voting to shortlist the most promising directions.

HMW Questions:

HMW ensure everyone contributes equally? HMW keep discussions on-track automatically? HMW make consensus visible in real-time?

05
Testing

Validating with Real Users

We conducted two rounds of usability testing. Round 1 used think-aloud protocol with 5 participants on the Figma prototype. Round 2 tested the working build with 4 participants in a simulated group discussion scenario.

"The turn timer was a bit stressful at first — but once I got used to it, it actually felt fair." — Usability test participant, Round 2

Key findings and changes made:

Issue Found

Timer countdown caused anxiety — users missed content while watching it.

Change Made

Moved timer to a subtle progress bar instead of a large countdown number.

Issue Found

Voting results were not visible until the end — felt disconnected.

Change Made

Added real-time vote tallies visible to the facilitator during the session.

06
Reflection

What We Learned

This project deepened our appreciation for the iterative nature of human-centric design. We entered with assumptions about what users needed — and research consistently challenged those assumptions in valuable ways.

What Worked Well

Early low-fidelity prototyping saved significant development time by catching structural issues before any code was written.

What We'd Do Differently

We'd recruit more diverse participants in research — our sample skewed toward CS students and may not represent all user groups.

Biggest Surprise

Users cared far more about psychological safety in discussions than about efficiency — a dimension we hadn't initially considered.

If We Had More Time

We'd explore AI-assisted facilitation and test whether automation of turn management feels empowering or controlling.
